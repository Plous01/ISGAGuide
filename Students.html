The guide to Dutchness

Students

The bee is important to the natural biodiversity of any country and also of the Netherlands, with around 30% of all our daily food relying on this insect. Students make Amsterdam buzz. And like the humble bee, the student population in Amsterdam is an important source of cultural and economic growth.

The International Student Network

The International Student Network (ISN) is an organisation that aims to help international students integrate into life in Amsterdam. They do this by organising many activities to help students meet and to help them make the most of their exchange. To participate in the activities you need to purchase an ESN card for €5, but this is nothing compared to the discounts you will receive.

Introduction week

Before the university semester begins, ISN organises an airport pick-up service for all international students. Buses take students to Science Park where they can pick up their house keys and register with the municipality of Amsterdam. During the rest of the week, several activities will be planned like ice skating, pub crawls, scavenger hunting and brunches. It is a great way for students to get to know each other and to get their first look at Amsterdam. In addition to this, ISN organises a final party at the end of semester to farewell the students.

Coco's outback

Every Wednesday night the ISN hosts a drinks party (borrel) for international students at Coco’s Outback, an Australian-themed pub on Rembrandtplein. There are cheap drinks and entry is free. What better way to entice students?

Culture nights

There are several culture nights throughout the year and each night focuses on a different country or region. These nights are for Dutch students going on exchange and international students from those countries, but everyone is welcome. ISN hosts a range of these nights to help students share their cultures. Traditional food is eaten and there are guest speakers to enlighten the group

Day trips

Throughout the year a series of day trips are planned. These trips are to other cities in the Netherlands and to other countries too. Trying herring in Volendam, eating waffles in Belgium, discovering the Netherlands’ politics in The Hague, and smelling tulips in Keukenhof, these are just a few of the trips ISN has organised.

Discover Holland weekend

There is an annual weekend trip to explore more of Holland. This trip uncovers the rich culture and nightlife of Groningen, the Wadden island of Schiermonnikoog, and the boat-filled waterways of Giethoorn.

Super trip

Each semester ISN organises a “Super Trip” to a few different cities. In the 2017 spring semester, they went through central Europe; to Vienna, Budapest and Bratislava. The trip was a huge success and was enjoyed by all. The group conquered 3 cities in 5 days, and although everyone was tired by the end it was well worth it. As well as the Super Trip, there are also smaller weekend trips planned throughout the year. You can visit the ISN website for more information or see page 115 of the guide!

Student population

Amsterdam has a sizeable student population. Together, the UvA and VU University Amsterdam have over 50,000 students. The Universities of Applied Sciences (hogescholen or hbo) combined have approximately 55,000 students. Economics is the most popular course for the hogescholen, while Behavioural and Social Sciences attract the most students at UvA and VU.

Higher education

There are two types of higher education institutions in the Netherlands, Universities and Universities of Applied Sciences. The latter prepares students for specific professions and tends to be more practically oriented than universities, which focus more on theory and generating knowledge through research. Generally speaking, an hbo degree provides its graduates with sufficient skills and knowledge to practise the profession they were trained for, without any additional training

Bachelor or master

In the last decade, the system of higher education has been drastically reorganised to fit in with the British or American model. This means that a university education has been subdivided into a broader, three-year bachelor’s degree and a one-year master’s degree. At hogescholen the bachelor’s degree takes four years while in some cases graduates will be able to continue their studies for one or two years for a master’s degree. The aim of this European reorganisation is to make the system of higher education more transparent throughout Europe and to make it easier to study in another country

Student facilities

All educational institutions try to provide all facilities necessary to enable their students to study to the best of their abilities. There are faculty libraries providing a quiet place for studying, computer rooms to work on papers and, of course, students can go to the departments with questions about the course programme or for help with a particular problem. If you are thinking about switching to another course, you could consult the student counsellor. Advice and information about course programmes is also available at the university information centre, where you can go with general questions or to make a personal appointment

Social contacts

A good balance between work and relaxation makes for better results in your studies. The more pressure you are under, the more important it is to unwind and relax. Fortunately, there are a million interesting ways to spend your free time

Student organisations

Hogescholen and universities have several clubs to keep students informed about course schedules and related subjects and to represent the students in decisions regarding the programme. Parties, trips and other social activities are sometimes part of the programme as well.

Student societies

To get to know people outside your own institute or field, you can join one of Amsterdam’s Dutch student organisations. These organise all kinds of activities, ranging from lectures and plays to parties. For most organisations, membership is required. The UvA together with the Hogeschool van Amsterdam (HvA) has a similar society for international students, called the International Student Network Amsterdam (ISN). The ESN section for Inholland University of Applied Sciences Amsterdam Diemen is called ESN Diemen. They will make you feel more at home in the Netherlands and help you make new friends. The general student union is the ASVA. They represent students’ interests at universities, hogescholen and in the political arena. They also organise courses, political debates and much more. You can always contact ASVA if you want to become an active member of the student union. For homosexual, bisexual< and transgender students<, A.S.V. Gay organises meetings and can serve as a safe space

ASDV Bonaparte office

ASDV Bonaparte is a student debating society that meets on Thursday nights between 08.00 pm to 01.00 am. If you would like them to organise an English language evening for you, send them an e-mail. Some universities have their own cafés, like Fest at Amstelcampus or the Hogeschool voor Economische Studies (HES) Café. These cafés have a great atmosphere and are good places to meet fellow students. Another reason to go there is the fact that they are usually not expensive

International student organisations

The major organisation providing support to foreign students is the Organisation for Internationalisation in Education (Ep-Nuffic).

Ep-Nuffic

Ep-Nuffic gives foreign students information about international courses and about the Dutch system of higher education, the practical, day-to-day aspects of living in the Netherlands, including information about residence permits, and relevant news articles and comments on the Netherlands via a newsletter which is distributed every two weeks

ISN Amsterdam

International Student Network (ISN) Amsterdam is a student-run organisation dedicated to help international students studying at the UvA and the HvA with making the most of their stay in the Netherlands. ISN is part of the Erasmus Student Network (ESN), an organisation with 505 local sections in 38 countries. They are here to ensure that you are going to have the time of your life studying abroad. ISN achieves this by organising great introduction days, parties, weekend trips, excursions and other cultural activities. This dynamic organisation promotes integration through the Dutch coaches who guide international students during the introduction period, as well as a sophisticated buddy system. Some classic examples of ISN activities include the Discover Holland weekend, Dutch movie nights, Dutch lessons and daytrips to the places in the Netherlands you must not miss. In addition to this, ISN even organises a number of international trips to popular European destinations such as Prague, Paris and London. With an ESN card you can join all the ISN activities. In addition to this, this card provides you with various great discounts at their weekly ‘borrel’ (drinks) and lots of museums, shops and restaurants. Check out ISN Amsterdam or fi nd ISN on Facebook to stay updated

ESN VU Amsterdam

ESN VU Amsterdam is the international student association of VU University Amsterdam. ESN wants to contribute to the experience of exchange students in Amsterdam by organising social events and establishing links with organisations in order to bridge the gap between students and fi rms. At the beginning of each semester, ESN organises the ISI Days (International Student Introduction Days), an introduction to Amsterdam. Other events can be weekly borrels (drinks), hitchhiking weekends, bowling nights and ice-skating trips, or even city trips and full-fl edged weekends into the great outdoors (in the Netherlands or abroad).
